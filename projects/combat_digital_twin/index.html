Combat Robotics Digital Twin

Overview

The backbone of this digital twin is NVIDIA IsaacSim, a robotics simulation platform built on the Omniverse ecosystem. While traditional CAD allows for static design, Isaac Sim utilizes the PhysX 5 engine to deliver hardware accelerated, real-time physics and high-fidelity GPU-based ray tracing. I developed this environment specifically to bridge the gap between "perfect" designs and the chaotic reality of the arena for my robot, Chippy. By importing Chippy's URDF model into this workspace, I can perform complex "What If" scenarios—testing drive, weapon torque, and structural integrity without having to physically build the robot. This turns unpredictable combat into a data-driven engineering challenge, allowing me to iterate on Chippy’s geometry and performance in a photorealistic, physics-accurate sandbox.

System Capabilities

Impact & Collision Simulations

I utilize high-fidelity physics to simulate weapon-to-robot impacts, allowing me to rigorously test and optimize components for maximum engagement efficiency. Traditionally, gathering this data requires entering a tournament and hoping to be paired against a specific robot archetype an expensive process that often yields only a single fight's worth of information. The simulation environment removes this limitation, enabling dozens of iterative battles and design tweaks without the cost of physical fabrication. A clear example of this is seen in the videos below: initially, a vertical spinner striking my forks would send my robot flying, but by decreasing the fork angle in the sim, I was able to find a geometry that wins that same engagement and keeps the bot grounded.

Traction & Drive Optimization

The sim is perfect for moving beyond "vibes-based" drive design. I recently used it to help Emburn, a beetleweight multi-bot, resolve traction issues. By simulating its long chassis with different wheel diameters and positions, we confirmed that a 4WD setup would provide the necessary pushing power and stability, eliminating the guesswork of its original 2WD design.

Dynamic Arm Force Analysis

Operating a heavy weapon arm changes the robot's center of mass and handling. Within the digital twin, I can see exactly how the forces of the arm effect the robot's movement and see if the robot arm has enough torque to selfright the robot

Current Developments

While the simulation is already a powerful tool for validation, I am actively working on expanding its capabilities:

  • Impact Mapping: Systematically mapping out the hit-zones for Chippy’s arm against various robot archetypes.
  • AI Opponent Integration: Developing an AI-driven opponent to create an unpredictable testing environment.
  • Transmitter Integration: Creating a bridge to allow control via a standard RC transmitter for driving practice.
